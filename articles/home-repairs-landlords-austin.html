5 Home Repairs Austin Landlords Should Never Put Off

By Levi Bernard, Aedex Anima LLC · Austin, TX

We do a lot of work for landlords and property owners across Austin, Cedar Park, Round Rock, and the surrounding area. The pattern we see over and over is the same: a small issue gets reported, it sits for a few weeks while the owner decides what to do, and by the time we show up it's three times the job it would have been.

These are the five repairs where delay consistently costs the most money.

1

Water leaks — any water leak

Water is the most destructive force in a home and the one most landlords underestimate. A slow drip under a sink, a toilet that runs, a shower with failing caulk — these don't stay small. Water finds paths through subfloor, into walls, and under tile. By the time you can see visible damage, the structural damage is usually already done.

In Austin's humidity, mold can start growing within 24–48 hours of water saturation. A leaky faucet might be a $150 repair. The same leak left two months turns into subfloor replacement, mold remediation, and potentially a tenant habitability issue — easily $2,000–5,000.

Texas law note: Texas Property Code requires landlords to make repairs that affect health and safety within a reasonable time after written notice. Water intrusion and mold typically qualify. Delaying isn't just expensive — it can create legal exposure.
2

AC problems in summer

Austin summers are brutal. An AC unit that's struggling — not cooling properly, freezing up, making noise — won't make it through July on its own. When AC fails completely in a rental during a Texas summer, you have a tenant emergency on your hands. Emergency HVAC calls cost significantly more than scheduled maintenance, and depending on how long the unit is down, you may owe a rent reduction.

If a tenant reports the AC is "not keeping up" or making unusual sounds, take it seriously immediately. A refrigerant recharge, a capacitor replacement, or a coil cleaning is a few hundred dollars. A failed compressor in August is a much bigger conversation.

The other thing we see constantly: clogged condensate drain lines. In Austin's humidity these back up, trip the safety float switch, and shut the unit off. This is a 30-minute fix we do regularly — but it becomes an emergency call when it happens at 9pm in August and the tenant has kids.

3

Caulk and weatherstripping failures

This one sounds minor but adds up in two ways: energy bills and pest entry. Old or cracked caulk around windows, doors, tubs, and showers lets air, water, and insects in. Tenants notice high utility bills, and in Texas those bills are already high. It also creates water intrusion paths around windows and doors that cause wood rot over time.

Recaulking a bathroom or resealing windows is inexpensive and fast. Left alone, you're looking at rot, water damage, and tenant complaints about roaches or other pests finding easy entry points into the home.

4

Smoke and CO detectors

Texas law requires working smoke detectors in rental properties. Carbon monoxide detectors are required for homes with gas appliances or attached garages. These aren't optional and they're not the tenant's responsibility to maintain — landlords are required to provide working detectors at move-in.

Detector batteries and units themselves fail over time. Detectors older than 10 years should be replaced regardless of whether they beep when you test them. A working detector saves lives. A missing or failed detector in a fire is a liability that no landlord wants to face.

We check these on every property visit. It takes five minutes and costs almost nothing to stay current.

5

Garage doors and exterior locks

A garage door that's off track, has broken springs, or reverses erratically is both a safety hazard and a security issue. Garage door springs are under serious tension and should only be replaced by someone who knows what they're doing — but the repair itself is straightforward and affordable compared to the cost of a door that injures someone or fails to secure the property.

Similarly, exterior door locks that are sticky, loose, or have worn latches should be addressed promptly. A tenant who can't easily secure their home is a tenant who calls you at 11pm, files complaints, or moves out at the end of their lease.

The real cost of deferred maintenance

Beyond the repair costs themselves, deferred maintenance affects tenant retention. Good tenants who feel like their concerns are ignored move out. Turnovers are expensive — lost rent, cleaning, painting, repairs, and leasing costs add up fast. A landlord who responds quickly and fixes things properly keeps good tenants longer. That's worth more than any individual repair cost.

We work with several property owners in the Austin area who call us for routine maintenance visits — a walkthrough every few months to catch small issues before they become big ones. If you manage multiple properties, it's worth thinking about.
